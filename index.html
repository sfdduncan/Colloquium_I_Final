Project Proposal?

Post-Body Archives is a multi-format, immersive design experiment that reclaims algorithmic tools for embodied resistance. Built from the conceptual foundation of Indigenous techno-futurism, posthumanist critique, and the figure of the decolonized cyborg, the project asks how we might use the very systems that flatten and erase us to make ourselves present again, on our own terms.

This work draws from interactive game design, photogrammetry, and cartographic practices to explore how technologies of computation can be reoriented toward refusal, memory, and agency. Rather than rejecting technology outright, it enacts a form of sabotage through syntax—intentionally misusing visual, spatial, and procedural systems to interrupt the logics of capture. It does not aim to fix surveillance systems or mapping protocols. It bends them. It uses them against themselves.

At its core, Post-Body Archives offers three interwoven provocations:

  • Awareness through interaction (Spatial Installation) — Using photogrammetry and monocular vision to visualize space while rendering people as indistinct figures, prompting reflection on how visibility, legibility, and presence are shaped—and how ambiguity can become a form of resistance.
  • Resistance through representation (Game Design) — Using game design as a critical art form, the project constructs an interactive archive of “missing” or impossible datasets—records of bodies, movements, and memories that refuse to be quantified.
  • Refusal through counter-cartography — Challenging what is mapped, who is represented, and how presence can be registered without being reduced.

Core Proposition: Sabotage Through Syntax

Post-Body Archives uses the logic of dominant systems—machine vision, 3D reconstruction, geospatial mapping—not to comply, but to expose. The algorithms are not rewritten to be better. They are misused on purpose. They glitch. They distort. They stutter. In that breakdown, something else becomes possible: a space where refusal can be felt in code, and where presence can register outside the limits of legibility.

This is where the decolonized cyborg takes shape—not as a seamless hybrid of body and machine, but as a figure that navigates systems with intention, asserting control over how they engage with technology. This cyborg is not optimized. It is relational. It is aware. It is resistant by design.

Ultimately, Post-Body Archives asks:

  • What does it mean to be uncomputable by choice?
  • How can we build systems that allow us to disappear when we want to?
  • What does it mean to reclaim authorship over how we are represented in space?
